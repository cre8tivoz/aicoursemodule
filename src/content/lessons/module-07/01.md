# Capstone build: ship your static mini-site

This **lesson** walks you through the **capstone** summative task described in full in [`assessments/capstone.md`](../../assessments/capstone.md). Read that document as the **authority** on what you must deliver; treat this lesson as a **friendly route map**—plain language, moderate reading load, and steps you can tick off.

The capstone is **not** a hurdle: you are **not** blocked from other materials if you have not submitted yet. It **is** the place where you **prove** judgement at AQF Level 5: you **evaluate** structure and accessibility (**CO-03**), you **apply** creative direction through your brief and prompts (**CO-04**), and you can **justify** hosting and tool choices (**CO-05**).

## What you are building (in one breath)

**One to three HTML pages**, linked together, **your** topic, **hand-authored** HTML and **CSS**, **deployed** to a public URL, with **Lighthouse Accessibility ≥ 90%** recorded on that **deployed** address. No framework is required. Your site does **not** need to use Eleventy—that is how **this course** is built, not a rule for your artefact.

If that list feels heavy, remember: you already practised **briefs**, **prompts**, **review loops**, and **reading code** in earlier modules. The capstone is those habits **without training wheels**.

## Stage 1 — Lock the brief (short)

Open your **Module 06** brief. If pages, must-haves, or non-goals are still fuzzy, **edit the brief first**. Scope creep is the main reason capstones slip: five pages of half-finished content loses to **two** pages that meet every row of the marking guide.

**Check:** Does your brief promise **1–3** pages only? If it promises more, **cut** or **merge** before you build.

## Stage 2 — Files and folders

Create a **simple** project folder on your machine, for example:

- `index.html` (required; home)  
- Optional: `about.html`, `contact.html` (or your own names—stay consistent)  
- `styles.css` (or similar single stylesheet name)  
- `images/` for photos (lowercase names reduce deploy surprises)

Link pages with **relative** URLs (`href="about.html"` not hard-coded `file://` paths). Test by opening from the folder **and** again after deploy—some hosts are sensitive to **case** in file names (`Photo.jpg` ≠ `photo.jpg`).

## Stage 3 — Structure before decoration

Build **skeleton** HTML first: `header`, `nav`, `main`, `footer` where they fit your content; **one** `<h1>` per page for the main topic; `<h2>` / `<h3>` for sections **without** skipping levels for “looks.” Put real words in—not permanent **lorem**—so you notice when navigation or headings feel wrong.

Run your **Module 03** checklist mentally or on paper: landmarks, heading order, links, images with meaningful **alt** (or empty alt for purely decorative images), keyboard path through nav. Fix **structure** before you chase **pixel-perfect** CSS.

## Stage 4 — CSS that supports reading

Choose a **small** palette: background, text, one accent, maybe a muted secondary. Check **contrast** between text and background; check **links** against their background. Increase **line-height** on body copy if paragraphs feel cramped. Use **flex** or **grid** if you like—keep layouts **responsive**: narrow your browser window and confirm nothing overlaps or disappears.

**Do not** remove **focus** outlines without replacing them with a **visible** custom focus style. Keyboard users need to see where they are.

## Stage 5 — Deploy early, deploy often

Pick a host you can **justify** in a sentence (**CO-05**): e.g. free tier, HTTPS, fits static files, matches workplace policy. **Netlify**, **Vercel**, and similar drag-and-drop or Git workflows are common; **your own server** is valid if you have access.

Upload or connect your folder **before** you feel “ready.” A broken deploy on day one teaches you the pipeline; a perfect local folder that never uploads teaches you **panic** on the due date.

After each meaningful change, **re-deploy** or let your host auto-build so the **live** URL always matches what you think you shipped.

## Stage 6 — Lighthouse on the live URL

Open your **deployed** site in **Chrome**, open **DevTools** → **Lighthouse**, run **Accessibility** (mobile or desktop per your provider’s habit—document what you used). **Save** the score: screenshot or written note with **date** and **Chrome version**.

If you are **below 90%**, read Lighthouse’s **specific** items first—often **contrast**, **names** for buttons/links, **heading** order, or **alt** text. Fix **one** class of issue, redeploy, re-run. Avoid random prompt dumps; **target** the failing check.

Remember: Lighthouse **supports** assessment; it does not replace **manual** checks—**Tab** through your site without a mouse once before you call it done.

## Stage 7 — Content and honesty

**Content** must be **original** and **appropriate** for a professional adult audience. Write in **your** words. For images, use your own photos or assets you have **rights** to use; say so in a small credit line if your provider expects it. Passing off others’ work as yours fails the spirit of the task even when tools make copying easy.

Be **honest** about AI assistance: many learners used AI for **drafts**; **you** still verify structure, fix errors, and own the final ship. A short note in your submission (“Draft assistance from [tool]; all structure checked by hand”) is professional, not a confession.

## Mapping to the S/NS rows (self-test)

Before you submit, line up your work against the **five** areas in [`assessments/capstone.md`](../../assessments/capstone.md): Structure/UX, Accessibility, Visual design, Deployment, Content. **Overall S** needs **every** row at **S**. If one row is weak, **fix that row** before polishing extras.

## Practice

**Task A — Evidence pack draft**

List exactly what you will submit: **URL**, **Lighthouse** evidence, optional **repo** link, **one or two sentences** on hosting choice (**CO-05**).

**Task B — Pre-flight table**

Draw a table with five rows (one per marking area). For each, write **one** line of **evidence** an assessor could verify from the live site.

**Task C — Failure rehearsal**

In **six sentences**, describe **one** realistic way a capstone could earn **NS** (e.g. broken deploy, Lighthouse 78%, missing `h1`). Then describe the **first** fix you would apply. This builds judgement without inviting self-sabotage.

## Key takeaways

- **Authority** lives in [`assessments/capstone.md`](../../assessments/capstone.md); this lesson is a **process** guide.  
- **Deploy early**; run Lighthouse on the **live** URL, not only on local files.  
- **S** means **all** rows satisfied—scope discipline beats feature bloat.
