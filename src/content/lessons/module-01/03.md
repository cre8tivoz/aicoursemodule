# Creative director: brief, taste, acceptance criteria

Many failures in small web projects are not “technology failures.” They are **intent failures**. The team never agreed what “done” meant. The page tried to speak to everyone and ended up speaking to no one. The design looked fine in a mock-up but did not answer the visitor’s first question.

In professional creative work, a **creative director** holds the line on intent: what this project is for, who it serves, and what quality looks like. In this course, **you** practise that role—even if you are a team of one. Your IDE and your AI tools are **instruments**. The brief is the score.

This lesson teaches you to write a **brief** that is good enough to hand to another human **or** to an AI assistant. That skill directly supports stronger prompts later in the course.

## Introduction

If you only remember one sentence from this lesson, remember this: **tools amplify direction.** Clear direction produces useful output faster. Vague direction produces confident nonsense faster.

That matters for professional adults because your time has a cost. Rework is expensive. A 20-minute brief can save hours of wandering prompts. It also protects you ethically: you are less likely to ship content that “sounds smart” but misses the assignment.

## Key concepts

### What a brief contains (minimum viable)

A practical brief answers:

- **Goal** — What outcome should the page support? (Inform, persuade, register interest, etc.)
- **Audience** — Who visits, and what do they already know?
- **Scope** — How many pages/sections? What must be included? What is explicitly out of scope?
- **Tone** — Friendly, formal, plain, bold—pick words a teammate would recognise.
- **Must-haves** — Contact details, legal notice, brand colour rules, accessibility intent.
- **Definition of done** — What would you check before saying “ship”?

You do not need a fancy template on day one. You need **specificity**.

### Taste is not “whatever looks nice”

**Taste**, in this course, means **consistent choices** that match the audience. It is not snobbery. A community bake sale page and a corporate compliance page should feel different. Taste is the difference between:

- “We used blue because blue is safe,” and  
- “We used blue because it matches the club’s banner and passes contrast checks.”

Taste also includes **inclusive choices**: readable fonts, enough spacing, language that does not exclude people.

### Acceptance criteria: the anti-mystery

**Acceptance criteria** are observable checks. They turn “looks good” into “meets these conditions.” Examples:

- “There is exactly one main topic in the `<h1>`.”
- “Primary contact method is visible without scrolling on a phone.”
- “All images have text alternatives where needed.”

You will refine technical criteria later. The habit starts now: **write checks you can actually perform**.

Here is a slightly richer example you could adapt for a workplace page:

- **Readable**: Body text is comfortable to read on a phone without pinch-zoom (you will learn common ways to check this as you study CSS).
- **Navigable**: Keyboard users can reach all interactive elements in a sensible order.
- **Honest**: No claims we cannot support; contact details match official records.
- **Scoped**: No pages or sections outside the agreed brief (prevents “scope creep” disguised as helpful AI).

Notice that some criteria are **about people**, not about tools. That is creative direction in practice.

### Why this powers AI tools

AI tools do not read your mind. They predict text based on patterns. If you say “make a website,” the model must guess your audience, your scope, and your standards. If you say “3 sections, hobby photography, mobile-first, plain language, no stock clichés,” you have reduced the guesswork.

That is why creative direction is not separate from “technical work.” It **is** the work that makes technical output usable.

## Example

### Side-by-side: vague prompt vs useful brief

**Vague prompt (weak):**

> “Make a website for my hobby.”

Problems:

- “Website” could mean one page or twenty.
- “Hobby” does not tell the model what to emphasise.
- There is no audience, no tone, no definition of done.

**Useful brief (stronger):**

> **Goal:** Help neighbours book a 1-hour intro session for a community garden club.  
> **Audience:** Adults in my suburb; moderate web skill; many use phones.  
> **Scope:** One scrolling home page with: intro, meeting times, location map link, contact email. No login.  
> **Tone:** Welcoming, plain language, no jargon.  
> **Must-haves:** Email contact in the footer; readable text sizes; avoid auto-playing media.  
> **Done means:** A new visitor can find **when** and **where** within 30 seconds on a phone.

You can paste the stronger brief into an AI tool **or** give it to a designer. That is the point.

> **Callout — Same task, less rework**  
> The brief does not eliminate editing. It reduces **random** editing.

## Practice

**Task — Five-bullet creative brief**

Draft a **five-bullet** creative brief for a **fictional** micro-site. Pick one:

- A hobby you do **not** personally do (so you invent details), or  
- A “dream business” landing page you might never build.

Each bullet must map to: **audience**, **scope**, **tone**, **must-haves**, **definition of done** (you can combine slightly, but keep five bullets total).

**Rules:**

- Use plain language.
- No links required in this draft.
- Keep it short—this is a brief, not an essay.

**Optional peer-style review:** Read your brief aloud. If you stumble, your audience might stumble. Revise once.

## Key takeaways

- Creative direction is **intent + constraints**, not “more prompts.”
- A strong brief reduces guesswork for humans and for AI tools.
- **Acceptance criteria** turn quality into checks you can repeat.
- Your role in this course is to practise being the person who holds the line on intent.
