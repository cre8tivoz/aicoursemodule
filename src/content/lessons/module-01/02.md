# AI as accelerator, not a substitute for judgement

People often talk about artificial intelligence (AI) as if it were a co-worker who never sleeps. In some ways, that image is fair. Modern tools can suggest HTML structure, rewrite sentences, and explain errors faster than many of us can search old forum posts. But the same tools can also **sound confident when they are wrong**. They can invent facts, mix up accessibility rules, or produce layout ideas that look fine on one screen and fail on another.

This lesson names that tension clearly. **AI can accelerate** your work. It does **not** replace your **judgement**: the professional responsibility to check facts, structure, and ethics before anything goes live.

## Introduction

At work, “ship fast” is often rewarded. So is “ship safe.” Those two goals can conflict. AI pushes toward speed. Your role—especially in a static site project—is to build a **repeatable habit**: generate, then **verify**, then improve.

In many teams, the person who clicks “publish” is visible. What is less visible—but equally real—is the chain of decisions that made the page trustworthy: someone checked names, dates, links, and whether the page could be used without a mouse. If you use AI to move faster, you are not removing that chain. You are **compressing draft time**, not compressing responsibility. That is why this lesson uses the word **judgement** on purpose. Judgement is the skill of stopping when something “looks fine” and asking what could go wrong for someone who is not sitting next to you.

You do not need a computer science degree to verify. You need:

- Curiosity when something looks “too tidy.”
- A simple checklist (you will deepen it later in the course).
- The humility to say, “I do not know yet,” and look it up.

## Key concepts

### Drafting vs deciding

**Drafting** is getting words and structure onto the page. **Deciding** is choosing what is accurate, kind, legal-enough for your situation, and accessible enough for your audience. AI is strong at drafts. Decisions still belong to people—especially when:

- The page names **prices**, **policies**, or **health** information.
- The page represents your **employer** or **clients**.
- The page must work for people who **do not use a mouse** or who **use assistive technology**.

### “Looks fine” is not a test

A generated page can look fine to **you** on **your** laptop. That is one narrow test. Accessibility and real-world use need more:

- Can someone tab through links in a sensible order?
- Do headings outline the page in a logical way?
- Is colour contrast readable for more users than just yourself?

You will learn more mechanics later. The concept here is: **verification is part of the job**, not a polish step at the end.

### Categories of risk (a plain-language map)

When AI writes HTML or copy for you, common risk areas include:

1. **Truth and updates** — Facts can be wrong or out of date. A opening-hours line might be plausible and still false.
2. **Structure** — Heading levels might skip in ways that confuse screen readers. Landmarks like `<main>` might be missing.
3. **Security and privacy** — AI might suggest embedding third-party scripts you should not use without review. It might also tempt you to paste sensitive internal data into a cloud tool (a topic for later modules).
4. **Tone and inclusion** — Language might be slightly off for your audience, or exclude people without you noticing.

None of this means “do not use AI.” It means: **use AI with a workflow**.

### Accountability

If your name or your team’s name is on the site, **you** are accountable for what ships. AI is a tool. Tools do not hold responsibility—people do. That is why this course talks about you as a **creative director**, not a typist.

### How this shows up in meetings (plain language)

You may need to explain your workflow to a manager who does not care about tools—only outcomes. A useful sentence pattern is:

- “AI helps me draft faster; I verify structure and facts before publish.”

If someone asks, “Can the AI do it end-to-end?” the professional answer is often: “It can draft, but **we** decide what is accurate and acceptable for our audience.” That answer protects your credibility and sets realistic expectations about review time.

## Example

### Vignette: hero section and heading order

**Situation:** You asked an AI tool for a landing page hero: headline, subheading, call-to-action button, background image.

**Draft output (simplified idea):** The AI returns a cheerful layout with a big visual and text. It uses a headline tagged as a second-level heading (`<h2>`) because the model “thinks” the logo is the top heading.

**Why that can be a problem:** Many pages should have a single clear **main** topic for accessibility. If the real topic of the page is “Volunteer clean-up day,” that topic often belongs in the first **top-level** heading (`<h1>`), not buried as `<h2>` because the model guessed wrong.

**What you do:** You adjust the heading level and page outline so the structure matches the meaning. You might also tweak the button text so it describes the action (“Register”) instead of a vague “Click here.”

**What this vignette shows:** AI gave you speed. You applied **judgement** to structure and language. The page is better—not because you typed faster, but because you **reviewed with intent**.

> **Callout**  
> Good judgement often looks boring: check headings, check links, check the words that matter.

## Practice

**Task — Three checks**

List **three things** you would **always** verify before publishing AI-written HTML for a **public** work-related page. For each item, add **one sentence** explaining **why** it matters to a real visitor or to your employer.

**Example format (do not copy verbatim):**

1. **Heading structure** — Because …  
2. **Contact details** — Because …  
3. **Third-party embeds** — Because …

**Stretch (optional):** Add a fourth item about **tone** (inclusive, on-brand) and when you would ask a human reviewer.

**Reminder:** This is practice for professional habit-building. The goal is not “perfect answers.” The goal is **specific** answers you could actually use next week.

## Key takeaways

- AI can **accelerate** drafting and explanation; it cannot outsource **accountability**.
- Verification should cover **facts**, **structure**, and **ethics**—not only visual design.
- A small, repeatable review habit beats a single heroic proofread right before launch.
- You are learning to combine speed with **judgement**, which is central to this course’s creative-director posture.
